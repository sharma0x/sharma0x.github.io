## Design Context

### Users
Technical professionals — engineering managers, senior engineers, and technical recruiters — evaluating Prince Sharma as a candidate, collaborator, or thought leader. They arrive to browse work (case studies, architecture deep-dives) and decide whether this person thinks at the systems level they need. Secondary audience: developers who read the blogs or watch Princebits on YouTube and want to go deeper.

### Brand Personality
**Precise, unhurried, technical.**

The site should feel like a well-typeset monograph — the kind of document where every margin and weight is intentional. Not cold, but restrained. Quiet confidence that comes from deep expertise, not from flashy animation. The work and the writing should do the talking; the interface gets out of the way.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Light theme. Editorial restraint.** Think of a printed technical journal or a well-designed bookplate — paper-white surfaces, strong typographic hierarchy, generous whitespace, and one restrained accent color used sparingly. No hero illustrations, no floating avatars, no animated stat counters, no scarcity badges. The page breathes. Section transitions are structural, not decorative.

**References for feel** (not literal copies):
- The quiet confidence of a well-set book — margins, rhythm, and weight do the work
- Technical journals where the content is the hero and the layout serves reading
- Sites where "simple yet detailed" means: few visual elements, but every element is precisely placed

**Anti-references:**
- Generic SaaS landing pages (hero + stats + CTA + testimonials)
- Gradient-heavy, neon-accented dark dashboards
- Template portfolios with card grids and icon-above-heading patterns
- Anything that could be mistaken for a Vercel/Linear clone

### Design Principles

1. **Content is the interface.** Typography, spacing, and hierarchy carry the design — not decorative elements. If a component doesn't serve reading or navigation, remove it.

2. **Earn every pixel.** No filler sections, no stock avatars, no animated counters. Every element exists because it communicates something specific about Prince's work. Stats are only shown if they're genuinely impressive and verifiable.

3. **Asymmetric restraint.** Left-aligned text with deliberate asymmetry. Generous margins. Break the grid only when it serves emphasis — and then, barely. The site should feel composed, not gridded.

4. **One accent, rare and sharp.** A single brand color (not blue or purple) used on interactive elements and key details. Everything else lives in the neutral spectrum, tinted toward the brand hue.

5. **Editorial pace.** The page scrolls like a well-structured article — clear sections with breathing room between them, not a marketing funnel. Headings announce territory; body text rewards reading.
